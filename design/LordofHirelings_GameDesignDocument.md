Game is called Lord of Hirelings
Genre is auto battler, rpg, town builder, incremental game. It is not an idle game: there is no offline or away progression, and every advance requires the player to act.
Engine is Godot 4.7.
Art
The horizon shows a decrepit and ruined town, but it slowly gets rebuilt as the player purchases upgrades. Its state depends only on the combined building levels (not upgrade pips): 1-5 total levels is ruined, 6-11 is the first partially rebuilt state, 12-18 is the second partially rebuilt state, and 19-25 is prosperous.
The general style is 2D pixel art. The characters do not animate as they walk but instead rotate slightly back and forth on the bottom center while squash and stretching. Most animations should be efficient 2D tweens driven through code rather than keyframes or sprites.

Gameplay Overview - 4 main sections of gameplay

The loop: each cycle begins at night. The player starts the day by interacting with the rooster, which crows loudly, brings the sun up, and causes people to show up at the inn looking for hiring. The rooster can only be used at night — crowing is what starts the day, and it cannot crow again until night returns. You hire them and they move into the city square. The player can optionally purchase upgrades to buildings during either town phase, day or night, until the bell is rung; a newly rebuilt or upgraded shop is immediately available to adventurers who qualify to buy from it. Everything costs gold, and the player taxes their adventurers twice over. Enemy gold drops go to the adventurers themselves, never to the player directly, but every time adventurers earn gold in the dungeon the player mints 10% of it as a tax-copy, and every time an adventurer buys something at one of the player's shops the player takes a 10% commission on the sale. Then they go to the dungeon, which has a bell next to its entrance; ringing the bell starts the call to arms and locks all town changes for that expedition; the bell only works during the day, after the rooster has crowed that day — there is no calling to arms at night. (The dungeon is an old mine — its entrance is a timber-braced mine portal set into rock at the edge of town.) If the player calls to arms while there are no adventurers in the town (for example because they ran out of money and could not hire anyone), 1-2 random level 1 adventurers will always show up in the city square for free, so the player can never get permanently stuck. You can move through the bounded town with a top-down position with WASD in the pre-dungeon raid gameplay; the player cannot leave the town. The camera follows the Lord, keeping him mostly centered until it reaches a town-map edge, where it stops. You will travel to different shop vendors to hire adventurers, buy items, and train. The town consists of exactly five buildings — the inn, the weapon shop, the armor shop, the jewelry shop, and the training grounds — plus the non-building interactables: the rooster, the treasury chest, the graveyard, the dungeon entrance, and its bell. Each building has a different upgrade tree, and each of the three equipment shops sells exactly one of an adventurer's three gear slots. When the expedition ends and the expedition summary is closed, night falls again: surviving adventurers head to the shops first if they can afford a gear upgrade, then go to bed or do other night activities, and the rooster becomes usable once more. The day counter advances with each expedition — finishing an expedition is what rolls the world into the next night. This is intentional: there is no way to end a day without running an expedition. Once the rooster has crowed, the only path back to night is ringing the bell and completing a dive — there is no "skip day" option.

Free fallback adventurers. When the no-adventurer fallback triggers, roll uniformly for one or two free level 1 adventurers. They use the same class and name generator as the day's hireable recruits, arrive with tier 0 gear and 0 personal gold, and are immediately and permanently added to the hired roster before formation. They cost nothing and cannot be sponsored at this point.

Interacting with things: nearly every interactable supports two input methods. You can left click the interactable and your character will automatically pathfind toward it and open the relevant UI once close enough, OR you can manually approach it and press the interact button. For example, to show the upgrade UI for a building you can left click the appropriate part of the building and your character will pathfind there and open the UI, or you can walk up to that same part of the building and press interact. Likewise, to view an adventurer's stats you can click them and your character will approach and pull up their UI once close enough, or you can approach them and press interact. There is no HUD in the town view; the only overlaid UI is the proximity prompt that appears when the player is near an interactable.

The player's gold is never shown as a persistent HUD element. Instead there is a treasure chest in the town: simply approaching it shows a proximity prompt reading "Treasury: X Gold" where X is the player's current gold. No interaction is required — the prompt is the readout — so the chest is the one interactable whose prompt displays information rather than an action.

You can left click heroes to view their stats in a panel. The hero's name is the panel title, with their epithet as a subtitle below it (e.g. title "Aldric", subtitle "The Unyielding"). Both randomly generated — see Name Generation. At the top left of the panel, hanging fully outside the panel's edge at the same vertical position as the title, is a circle containing the hero's class icon, with the hero's level shown in a shield banner directly below the circle. At the top right, also outside the panel's edge, is a matching circle containing a gold bag icon, with a number below it indicating the adventurer's personal wealth (gold is shown here and is not listed among the stats). A line separator then their stats which consist of a stat icon, a “:”, then the stat as a number or percent. The first stat row is HP, Power, Morale. The second row is HP Regen, Morale Regen. All remaining stats follow after. Then a separator labeled "Inventory" followed by the hero's items where each item is shown as an icon + item name label. Every adventurer has exactly three inventory entries — their Weapon, their Armor, and their Jewelry — always all three present, since everyone is wearing at least tier 0 in each slot. Each adventurer equips exactly one weapon, one armor (a chest piece: plate, robes, leathers, and so on), and one piece of jewelry (a ring, an amulet, or a belt). There are no consumables, no potions, and no fourth slot; the inventory list is always exactly three rows. Then a separator and an activity section describing what the adventurer is currently doing: hireable adventurers show "Looking for work", hired adventurers show their current town activity (e.g. "Waiting around", "Chatting with Aldric"). For hireable adventurers there is then another separator followed by their class name and class description, both center aligned, and below that 2 buttons labeled "Hire (X Gold)" and "Hire and Sponsor (X Gold)" where X is the cost. Hire and Sponsor is the same as hire, but directly gives the adventurer money so they can buy better equipment from your own shops immediately.

Hired adventurers wander around the town and do things that make sense for themselves depending on how much money they have. If they can afford their next equipment upgrade they go buy it from the appropriate shop. If they don't have enough money for the next upgrade they instead do something flavorful like waiting around, eating, chatting with other adventurers, working out, bathing, training, playing with the dog, or visiting the graveyard. Sleeping is a night-only activity: at night adventurers with nothing left to buy go to bed or do quieter night activities, while during the day they never sleep. An adventurer visiting the graveyard stands at a random grave and says something like "I miss you, X" where X is the name of the dead adventurer buried there. Their current activity is what shows in the activity section of their stat panel.

Once you call to arms the adventurers randomly form up to 3 parties of 1-3 adventurers each. Ringing the bell also ends the day's recruitment: every unhired recruit at the inn immediately walks off screen, and a recruit who is walking away can no longer be hired — from the moment the call to arms begins, hiring is impossible until the next day's crow. Formation works like this: shuffle the list of hired adventurers, then the first 9 of them (or all of them, if there are fewer than 9) each join one of the 3 parties, picked uniformly at random among the parties that still have fewer than 3 members. So 5 adventurers can end up as 3-1-1, 3-2, or 2-2-1 — a party can end up empty, but never larger than 3. An empty party is treated as if it does not exist: it gets no row in the dungeon dive and no column in the expedition summary — both screens only ever show the non-empty parties. Adventurers beyond the first 9 stay in town as reserves; they are who the Kick button swaps in. If the player owns the Guild Champion upgrade from the inn, formation is seeded before the shuffle: the highest-level adventurer in town (ties broken randomly) is placed into a party chosen uniformly at random, and only then does the normal shuffle-draft fill the remaining 8 slots from everyone else. This guarantees your best adventurer is never benched. Clicking a hero during this phase opens their normal hero stat panel, but with one extra section at the bottom that fits on a single horizontal row: a "Party:" label, a Kick button, a Swap button, and an "X/Y Actions Today" label. Y is the number of party actions the player can take per day: 2 by default, raised to 3 by purchasing the War Table upgrade at the inn. X counts down as actions are spent, and each Kick or Swap costs 1 action. The counter resets each day. Swap button will randomly swap that adventurer with another adventurer in a different party. Kick will swap that adventurer with an adventurer that is not in a party. The Kick button is disabled whenever there are no reserves to swap in — since formation drafts up to the first 9 adventurers into parties, reserves exist only when more than 9 adventurers are in town. If Swap is pressed when there is no valid target (for example only one party is non-empty), it is a no-op: nothing happens and no action is consumed. Once the player is satisfied with the parties formed they can move to the dungeon and hit enter to trigger the next stage of the game.

Entering the dungeon after party formation uses the normal click/Interact action at the dungeon entrance. The earlier wording "hit enter" describes this transition; it is not a separate Enter-key binding.

Kick's replacement roll. By default Kick pulls a uniformly random reserve. If the player owns the Seasoned Replacements upgrade from the inn, the roll becomes weighted: every reserve starts at weight 1, and a reserve whose level is within ±1 of either the target party's average level or the target party's highest-level member is tripled to weight 3. The party's average and highest are computed after the kicked adventurer has been removed — that is, they describe the party the replacement is actually joining. If removing the kicked adventurer leaves the party empty there is no average or highest to compare against, so every reserve stays at weight 1. Averages are fractional and compared as such, so a reserve at level 4 matches a party averaging 4.7.

In the dungeon dive the screen is split into equal rows, one per non-empty party — up to 3; an empty party gets no row. Each row shows its party's 1-3 character portraits in the top left of the row. Battle presentation follows mockups/dungeon-hud.html: every combatant shows a red HP bar beneath their sprite, adventurers additionally show a blue morale bar (enemies are immune to morale, so they have none), a yellow ▼ marker hovers over whichever character's turn it currently is, and floating combat text pops over the target of each action — damage numbers, "MISS", "CRIT -X!", and green +X heals. Each defeated encounter leaves an ✕ marker behind on the row. A cowering adventurer huddles in place with a visible cower indicator, a fleeing adventurer turns and runs back off the left edge of the row, and a dying character flashes red and falls over before fading out. The only overlay UI during the dive is the Horn of Retreat button in the top right, and it only appears once the Horn of Retreat has been purchased from the inn's upgrade tree; pressing it makes every party flee immediately, ending the expedition. Otherwise there is no overlay UI: no battle counter, no boss indicator, and no gold, dungeon level, or day display. Each party moves left to right through its row, exposing the fog of war as it advances. Enemies — except for the boss at the end of the level — show up and intercept the adventurers as they progress; the turn-based auto battle is fought then and there, and after winning the party moves forward again, repeating this until they finally reach the boss. On the first expedition only — day 1, when every party necessarily runs dungeon level 1 — these enemies never roll gold traits: no Gilded or Hoarder enemy appears in the opening dive. The very first run is deliberately kept clean and predictable so it lands on the balance anchors (the first kill worth exactly 1 XP, a fresh party's survivability) rather than being skewed by a fat, tanky Hoarder or a jackpot Gilded drop; from day 2 onward enemies roll gold traits normally on every dungeon level, including level 1. Pacing intent: a single battle should last about 15 seconds when the two sides are very evenly matched and low-DPS, and walking takes about 4 seconds as a party first enters the level and again between each fight. The implementation constant that serves that intent is a fixed 0.6 seconds per character turn regardless of what they do, which puts the first level 1 battle around 6-9 seconds, a genuinely even low-DPS matchup around 25 seconds, and the dungeon level 4 boss fight around 18-36 seconds, so a full dungeon level 1 clear runs about 65 seconds. The 15 second intent sits between the first two of those, so 0.6 seconds brackets the anchor rather than hitting it exactly; the constant wants tuning once real battles can be measured. There is no speed control and at these lengths none is needed, because the Horn of Retreat remains the player's only input during a dive. Defeating the boss exposes the stairs down to the next dungeon level, or, if this is the final level of the dungeon, the party celebrates on the spot because the game has been won. The stairs are a flavorful visual depiction of the unlock only — the party does not descend them. After defeating the boss and unlocking the stairs the party returns home; a party always tackles exactly one dungeon level per expedition. A party keeps going until every member has fled or died, they complete the level, the stalemate rule forces them out, or the player sounds the Horn of Retreat. If a row's party wipes or flees, that row continues to exist for the rest of the dive but nothing further happens in it visually. The dungeon has 4 levels. Each dungeon level consists of 4-5 regular fights of increasing difficulty, culminating in a boss battle as the final battle: dungeon levels 1 and 2 run 4 fights and then the boss, and dungeon levels 3 and above run 5 fights and then the boss. So a level is 5 battles deep at dungeon levels 1-2 and 6 battles deep at dungeon levels 3-4, which is what makes the back half of the dungeon a longer grind as well as a harder one. Only 1 party needs to defeat the boss, at any point, for that level to be completed; completing a level unlocks the next dungeon level for all future expeditions (dungeon level 1 is always unlocked). Parties traversing the same dungeon level fight completely independent copies of it — separate enemy spawns, separate boss, separate once-per-fight ability flags — so nothing one party does can affect another party's row; two parties on the same level each fight their own Lich. Each party tackles the hardest unlocked dungeon level they think they can accomplish. This is judged purely by a minimum average party level per dungeon level — there is no upper bound, because an over-leveled party should always be willing to take on the hardest thing it can reach rather than being pushed back down to an easier level. Dungeon level N requires an average party level of at least 2N - 1: level 1 requires average level 1 (so it always fits), level 2 requires average level 3, level 3 requires average level 5, level 4 requires average level 7. Parties pick the highest unlocked level whose minimum they meet, so different parties can traverse different dungeon levels in the same expedition during the ramp-up. Once every party averages level 7 or higher, all parties will run dungeon level 4. The player is expected to beat the 1st level in 3 days, 2nd level in 5 days, 3rd level in 9 days, and 4th level in 14 days assuming they upgrade as much as possible between days in an optimal way. These day targets are per level, not cumulative. Each dungeon level has its own biome: level 1 is a grassy field with some trees, like a gentle forest; level 2 is a swamp, so it looks like a swampy forest; level 3 is an underground undead crypt crawling with skeletons, zombies, and a lich; level 4 is a volcanic, fiery lava area overrun by demons. When the player conquers the 4th and final dungeon level they have won the game. Winning unlocks endless mode. The win is announced with a simple text panel that appears the moment the winning expedition's summary is closed: the title is "You Win!", followed by a short description congratulating the player on conquering the dungeon and explaining what they can do now that they have beaten the game — keep hiring, upgrading, and sending parties into the same dungeon in endless mode, where each further expedition that fully clears level 4 raises the endless tier, making enemies tougher and their drops richer — and a close button. When it closes, night falls as normal. Endless mode does not add any new dungeon levels — there are only ever 4. The player simply keeps sending parties down the same dungeon, and each expedition in which at least one party fully clears level 4 increments the endless tier by exactly 1, which makes the enemies there tougher and their drops richer. The increment is per expedition, not per party: three parties all clearing level 4 on the same day is still only +1 tier, matching the rule that only 1 party has to beat the boss for the level to count as completed.

Combat Mechanics
Turn order is rolled fresh at the start of every round. Each character rolls a d20 and adds their speed stat; the highest total goes first, descending. Ties are broken in this order, stopping at the first rule that separates them: the character with the higher speed stat goes first; then the enemy goes before the player's character; then the character with more buff/debuff abilities goes first; then the character with more damage/power goes first; then pick randomly.

On their turn a character will generally use their ability to attack, heal, aoe attack, or defensively attack. Enemy attacks deal both HP damage and their listed morale damage. Adventurer attacks deal HP damage only: enemies are immune to morale, so player-side morale damage has no mechanical purpose and is not a hero stat. If a character's health hits 0 they die. Adventurers that die will get added to the graveyard unless the graveyard is full in which case they will overwrite the oldest grave. A fleeing character safely goes back to the town square.

Ability cadence is per battle by default. Any ability described as firing "every Nth turn" counts that character's own turns within the current battle, and the counter resets to zero at the start of every battle — so a Captain always opens a fight with two normal attacks before his first Rallying Horn, and a Demon Prince always opens with two normal attacks before his first Eruption. Once-per-fight triggers like the Lich's Raise Dead likewise re-arm at the start of each battle. This is the default for every ability unless that ability explicitly says otherwise.

Status effects. Every buff and debuff specifies its duration in turns and its stacking behavior. A duration ticks down by 1 at the end of the affected character's turn, including a turn spent cowering, and expires at 0. An effect may stack as separate instances, increase the magnitude of one shared instance, or refuse to stack and instead refresh its duration; the individual ability defines which of these it uses. Current ability-specific rules are in LordofHirelings_BalanceNumbers.md.

Combat event order. At the start of each round, roll initiative for every living combatant currently in the battle. Resolve those turns in order; a combatant that dies or flees before its scheduled turn simply skips it. A summon enters the battle immediately but cannot act until the next round. On a landed attack, apply HP damage, then resolve any immediate on-damage effect: lifesteal heals for actual HP damage dealt, a Lich raises minions only if it crossed below half HP and is still alive, and a death immediately deals its 2 morale damage to every surviving party member. At the end of the round, resolve enemy end-of-round effects such as the Swamp Troll's regeneration, then check the party's stalemate counter. A fleeing adventurer causes no death-morale effect.

Morale, panic, cowering, and fleeing. Morale never causes an enemy to flee — enemies deal morale damage but are immune to it and always fight to the death. Only the player's adventurers can panic. At the start of an adventurer's turn, if their current morale is below half their max morale, they roll a panic check. Let m be current morale divided by max morale:

Panic chance = (0.5 - m) * 2, clamped to 0-100%

So panic chance is 0% at half morale and rises linearly to 100% at zero morale. If the panic check succeeds, roll a second check at the same odds: on a success the adventurer flees the dungeon outright, and on a failure they cower instead. This means the chance to flee on a given turn is the square of the panic chance, so cowering dominates at moderate morale and fleeing takes over as morale approaches zero, and an adventurer at 0 morale always flees on their next turn.

Cowering is a status effect: the adventurer panics and skips their turn, then restores 20% of their max morale afterward. Cowering is therefore self-correcting — a cowering adventurer claws their way back toward the panic threshold on their own — which makes low morale a spiral the party can sometimes pull out of rather than an automatic loss.

Stalemate rule. If a party deals no damage at all for 3 consecutive rounds, the whole party flees, even if their morale is perfectly healthy. This is measured as damage dealt by the party, not as the enemy's net health change: if the party is landing hits but the enemy out-heals them, the counter resets and the rule does not trigger. Attacks that miss, and attacks fully absorbed by a guard, count as no damage. The counter resets to zero the moment any party member deals at least 1 damage. This exists so a party with no damage output — a lone Cleric, or a party of nothing but Clerics, since the Cleric has no attack — cannot lock a battle forever.

Enemies implicitly have 0% guard and 0% crit chance — blocking and critical hits are adventurer-side mechanics. And because guard is checked before the crit roll in the attack resolution order, a blocked attack never rolls a crit at all (it still deals its full morale damage).

Minions. Some enemy abilities summon reinforcements mid-battle; a summoned enemy carries the minion tag. Minions never drop gold, and they award XP at a 50% penalty, rounded down. Within a single party's expedition, a given summon only pays XP once: if the summoner's ability fires again after its minions have already been defeated that expedition, the re-summoned minions award no XP at all. The Lich's Raise Dead skeletons are currently the only minions in the game.

When an adventurer dies, all of their equipment is lost along with them, and so is any personal gold they were carrying. An adventurer who flees keeps both, along with all XP gained from enemies defeated before they fled.

Class roles (gameplay). Adventurers generally have a single target attack unless noted otherwise.
Knight: more defensive abilities and tanky.
Captain: morale healing and boosts his comrades' damage. Ok health, tankiness, and damage.
Berserker: high damage. Ok health and tankiness.
Mage: med-high AOE attack. Low defense and low health.
Rogue: high single target attack. Ok health and ok defense.
Cleric: does not have an attack; focuses on healing and defensive buffs. Ok health and ok defense. The Cleric's power stat drives the size of its heal rather than a damage roll, so the Cleric scales off exactly the same things every other class does — levels, weapon tier, Sharper Steel, Sparring Dummies — and its "weapon" (a mace, censer, or reliquary) is a real purchase rather than dead gold.

Adventurers have different stats. Health, max morale, morale regen, power which determines the damage dealt by an attack — and, for the Cleric, the size of its heal instead — gold, hp regen, speed which is added to a d20 roll to determine turn order each round, guard % which determines the chance to block an attack (max 75% for all characters), armor which reduces damage by the amount of armor the adventurer has, evasion which is pitted against the attack accuracy stat of the enemy, crit chance which on attack gives a chance to deal crit damage bonus, crit damage bonus which defaults to 50% meaning the crit attack deals 1.5x the damage, Accuracy which determines if the attack lands depending on the enemy’s evasion. The formula below is used to calculate the chance to Hit, where AA is the total Accuracy Rating of the attacker and DE is the total Evasion Rating of the defender.
Chance to Hit = AA * 1.25 * 100 / ( AA + DE * 0.3 )
The formula's result can exceed 100%, but any value above 100% does nothing extra — the chance to Hit is capped at 100%. The chance to Hit is also floored at 5%, meaning the chance to Evade a Hit can never exceed 95%.
Evasion and Accuracy are raw ratings, not percents. Every enemy's evasion and accuracy is defined by a formula that extrapolates to any level (see LordofHirelings_BalanceNumbers.md), so a reference enemy exists at every level even though the game only ever ships four dungeon levels. When displaying either stat as a percent in the UI, substitute the missing side of the formula with the corresponding rating of a reference grunt — the most common enemy in the game — drawn from the dungeon level that adventurer would actually be sent to. That reference dungeon level is R = clamp(floor((L + 1) / 2), 1, 4) for an adventurer of level L, which is the inverse of the 2N-1 party gate: a level 1 adventurer is measured against dungeon level 1 enemies, a level 3 against dungeon level 2, a level 7 or higher against dungeon level 4. So the displayed percentages read as "your odds against the enemies you will actually face," and they stay meaningful past level 7 and all the way through endless mode instead of being measured against enemies that do not exist. Displayed evasion percent uses the reference grunt's accuracy as AA and the adventurer's own evasion rating as DE, then reports 100% minus the resulting chance to Hit. Displayed accuracy percent uses the adventurer's own accuracy rating as AA and the reference grunt's evasion as DE. Both use the same 5%-100% clamp as the underlying formula. Note that because of the formula's 1.25 multiplier, displayed accuracy sits at a flat 100% until the reference enemy's evasion climbs above roughly 83% of the adventurer's accuracy rating, which is most of the early game — that is expected, and it is why accuracy is a late-game stat.
Crits are all or nothing: an attack either crits or it does not. An AOE attack rolls its damage and its crit a single time and applies that one result against every target; each target still resolves its own accuracy, guard, crit-downgrade, and armor checks individually. The only exception is an attack that explicitly fires multiple projectiles, where each projectile rolls its own damage and crit — no current ability does this; the rule exists for future content. Crits also use accuracy: if an attack is a critical hit and the enemy fails to evade it, evasion is checked a second time at the same odds — if that second evasion check succeeds, the hit is downgraded to a non-critical hit (it still lands, just without the crit bonus).

Economy
The player starts with 19 gold. Rebuilding a ruined building costs 10 gold; upgrading a building's level costs 40 gold for level 1 to 2 and scales up sharply from there. Hiring a fresh level 1 adventurer costs 7-9 gold, and each adventurer level beyond 1 increases the hiring cost by 4-7 gold. Both rolls happen once, at the moment the adventurer is generated, and the resulting number is that adventurer's fixed base cost for as long as they stand at the inn: a level 1 recruit rolls randint(7, 9), and an elite arriving at level K rolls randint(7, 9) plus K-1 independent randint(4, 7) rolls. The hire + sponsor option doubles the hiring cost.

There are two separate purses. The player has a treasury, and every adventurer has their own personal gold shown on their stat panel. All gold dropped by enemies in the dungeon goes to the adventurers, never to the player directly. The player has two income streams, both of them taxes on their adventurers.

The tax-copy. Whenever adventurers earn gold in the dungeon, the player mints 10% of it, rounded down. This is a copy, not a cut: the 10% is created out of nothing and added to the treasury, and nothing is subtracted from the adventurers, who keep every coin they picked up. If the adventurers earn 57 gold in total, the player gains 5 and the adventurers still have all 57.

The tax-copy is computed on the total gold earned across all adventurers on the expedition, and rounded down exactly once, at the end. It must not be floored per drop: a grunt drops 0-2 gold, and 10% of that floors to 0 every single time, so a per-drop rounding would pay the player nothing at all on dungeon level 1. Accumulate first, then take 10%, then floor.

The sales commission. On top of that, every purchase an adventurer makes at one of the player's shops pays the player a 10% commission, minimum 1 gold, rounded up. This one is a cut of the vendor's revenue, not a surcharge — the adventurer still pays the listed price. A tier 1 item at 8 gold pays the player 1; a tier 5 item at 128 pays 13.

The loop is therefore: your adventurers get rich in the dungeon and you skim them coming and going — once when they earn it, again when they spend it. Because the tax-copy does not depend on owning any shop, the player always has income from day 1 and can never be locked out of the economy, which is why every building except the inn can safely start ruined. Sponsored gold is not recovered when it is spent — sponsoring hands the adventurer real money that is gone from your treasury — but it makes them stronger, so they earn more and you tax-copy more, and their spending pays commission like anyone else's.

Gold counted toward the tax-copy remains eligible even if the adventurer who earned it later dies and loses their purse. The total tax-copy is awarded when the expedition resolves, after all eligible gold has been summed and floored once.

Fractional gold never exists in a purse — the player's treasury and every adventurer's wallet are always whole numbers. Two upgrades nevertheless grant fractional-percentage benefits (Recruit Discount and Recruiter's Cut), and both resolve the same way: a hidden floating-point credit accumulates across purchases and pays out in whole gold as soon as it reaches 1. The exact accumulator rules and worked examples live in LordofHirelings_BalanceNumbers.md; the important part for design is that a small percentage never rounds away to nothing, it just takes several purchases to materialize, and the player sees it as an occasional "this one is 1 gold cheaper" rather than as a fraction.

Balance
Specific stat numbers should be determined through playtesting; a first draft is written up in LordofHirelings_BalanceNumbers.md. Three anchor points to tune against. First, a level 1 Mage should deal 1-3 AOE damage as a med-low to med-high damaging character. Second, a Captain should be able to take 6-7 attacks (by dodging some of them) from the very first level 1 enemy encounter before dying. Third, the very first enemy the player ever kills should award exactly 1 XP to each living party member — XP is awarded per living member rather than split, and 1 is the unit the whole XP curve is denominated in, so the first kill is what sets the scale for every reward in the game.

There is a post dungeon expedition summary panel which shows the party adventuring results. The title is “Expedition Summary Day X” where “X” is replaced with the current day. The day is just a count of how many expeditions have been made. So the first expedition is day 1. Second is day 2. Etc. The panel is sized to about 80% of the window both vertically and horizontally, and the results area has a vertical scroll bar in case the content doesn't fit on screen. There is a column for each party that went out (an empty party never existed, so it gets no column — the panel shows one column per non-empty party); all columns are the same width, and each column always reserves enough vertical space for 3 adventurers. At the top of each column, above the party results, is a visual of the dungeon level that party traversed (see dungeon level biomes). One by one left to right each column animates the results. The xp bar fills up for each party member according to how much xp they earned. To the left of the xp bar is the member's current level, and to the right of the bar is the next level, both shown as a number on a shield banner in the same style as the hero stat panel's level indicator. If they level up it visually showcases that they advanced to the next level and their level increases. To the right of the xp bar (after the next-level banner) their earned gold is shown as a pile of coins that looks progressively more full, luxurious, and rich the more gold was earned. This is that adventurer's own personal gold earned on the expedition — the money going into their pocket to spend at your shops — not the player's gold, since the player earns nothing directly from the dungeon. The coin pile is drawn: 1 visual coin per 3-6 gold earned (minimum 1 coin if any gold was earned), maxing out at 100 visual coins. If a member earned 0 gold no gold is shown at all. Dead adventurers cannot earn gold, so they never show gold, and they do not show an xp bar either. If that adventurer dies we animate that they died by rotating their character sprite to be lying down and tint it red + blood splatter. There is also a status text that says “Fled”, “Heroically cleared the way to X” where X is the next level, “Conquered the final dungeon”, or “Murdered by a X” where X is the enemy that killed them (it can also replace murder with Slain/Killed/Defeated/Crushed/Devastated/Demolished/Destroyed” below their name and appears to denote the final result of that character. Finally a close button to close the panel.

Levelling up happens here and nowhere else. XP accumulates silently during the dive but has no effect while the party is in the dungeon: an adventurer's stats are frozen at the values they walked in with for the entire expedition, and every level gained is applied at the moment the expedition summary animates it. This keeps a single dive's difficulty knowable from the party that entered it, and it is why the summary is where the level-up is celebrated. Because an adventurer can gain several levels at once — a full dungeon level 1 clear is already worth nearly two levels to a fresh recruit — the xp bar animation loops: the left banner starts at the level the adventurer entered the expedition with and the right banner at one above it, the bar fills to full, both banners increment by one, the bar snaps back to empty, and it repeats until the leftover XP is placed, leaving the bar resting at the adventurer's progress toward their next level. Adventurers who fled still level up, since they keep all XP earned before fleeing. Dead adventurers show no bar and no banners.

Inn
This shop has a section where adventurers can be hired. Left click an adventurer to open the hiring UI (your character will pathfind to them and it opens once close enough), or approach them and press the interact button. It will display their name + cost + class + level.

Adventurers only appear at the inn once the rooster has been used that day; the rooster is what summons them, and the inn is where they gather. Each recruit's class is rolled uniformly at random from the six classes, independently per slot, so duplicates are common and a given day can easily produce three Rogues. Elite Recruits is also rolled independently per recruit slot: each pip gives that recruit a further 5% chance to be elite, and an elite gains 1d3 levels, arriving at level 2-4. Recruits who are not hired do not persist: they leave when night falls, and the next morning's crow generates a fresh set from scratch. The town holds at most 100 hired adventurers; hiring is disabled while at that cap.

The inn also has a door like every other shop where you can upgrade the inn itself. The inn starts the game at level 1 and does not need to be rebuilt. You can upgrade the inn level itself which unlocks additional upgrades, upgrade the hero capacity so each day you have more heroes, improves the chance of an elite version of a hero for recruitment, reduces the cost of recruitment, purchase the War Table which raises the player's party actions during call to arms from 2 to 3, purchase the Horn of Retreat, purchase Seasoned Replacements and Guild Champion which together stop a large roster from diluting your parties, and purchase Recruiter's Cut which sends every new hire into town with pocket money.

Seasoned Replacements, Guild Champion, and the roster problem. Formation drafts 9 adventurers from a shuffle of everyone hired, which means that without help, growing the roster actively makes your parties worse: a level 7 veteran and a level 1 recruit hired last night have exactly the same odds of being drafted, so every extra body dilutes the nine slots that matter. Guild Champion (inn level 2) fixes one slot outright by seeding the highest-level adventurer into a party before the shuffle. Seasoned Replacements (inn level 1) makes the player's Kick actions land better by tripling the weight of reserves whose level suits the party they are joining. Neither is a complete answer and that is intentional: with a roster of 30 and both upgrades bought, the player fields roughly 4.8 veterans out of 9 rather than 2.7, which is a real improvement but still under two-thirds. The roster is capped at 100 hired adventurers to contain that dilution. There is no roster browser, retirement action, or direct party assignment; random formation plus 2 base actions (3 with War Table) remains the intended degree of player control.

An elite recruit is simply an adventurer who starts at a higher level than normal. Recruits are level 1 by default; an elite rolls 1d3 extra levels, arriving at level 2-4, and costs correspondingly more to hire per the hiring cost rule. Elites are the only way higher-level adventurers ever appear at the inn.

The Horn of Retreat is a one-off inn upgrade rather than a multi-tier one. Buying it permanently adds a single button to the top right of the dungeon delve UI which, when pressed, causes every party currently in the dungeon to flee at once, ending the expedition immediately. It is the player's only direct input during a dive.

Recruiter's Cut is a one-off inn upgrade available from inn level 1. Once bought, every adventurer the player hires arrives carrying personal gold worth 10% of their pre-discount base cost — sponsored or not. It resolves through the fractional-credit accumulator, so a string of 8 gold hires pays out 1 gold to roughly every second recruit rather than paying nothing at all. Sponsored hires stack it on top of the sponsorship, so a sponsored recruit effectively arrives with 110% of the sponsor amount. The point is to shorten the dead stretch where a fresh recruit has nothing and can buy nothing, and to feed the shop commission a little earlier.

Recruit Discount reduces what the player pays, never what the adventurer receives. The sponsorship amount handed to a sponsored recruit is always their pre-discount base cost, so discounting recruitment makes hiring cheaper without quietly nerfing sponsorship.

The three equipment shops. Gear is split across three separate buildings — the weapon shop, the armor shop, and the jewelry shop — one per gear slot. Each is its own building with its own upgrade tree, each starts ruined and must be rebuilt for 10 gold, and each sells only its own slot. An adventurer wears exactly one weapon, one armor, and one piece of jewelry, so the three shops never compete for the same slot; they compete for the adventurer's purse.

Every shop gates its tiers the same way, and the building's level is the only thing that does it: a level 1 shop stocks tiers 1 and 2, a level 2 shop adds tiers 3 and 4, and a level 3 shop adds tier 5, which is the maximum. There is no separate "unlock tier" upgrade line — levelling the building is the unlock. Every adventurer arrives wearing tier 0 in all three slots, and a freshly rebuilt shop already sells tier 1 with no further investment, so every rebuild immediately does something. Until a given shop is rebuilt, its slot simply stays at tier 0 and adventurers bank the money they would have spent there, then buy in bulk the day it opens. Because the shops are independent, the player chooses which slot their roster gets to improve first, and that ordering is one of the real strategic decisions in the town.

Levels 4 and 5 of each shop unlock that shop's two shop-wide lines, which apply to every adventurer in town at once but only to gear of tier 1 or higher. The split is thematic: the weapon shop's lines are offense, the armor shop's are defense, and the jewelry shop's are morale.

The weapon shop sells the weapon slot. A weapon raises power, which is what drives damage — and for the Cleric, whose power sizes its heal instead, it drives healing. The two tanky classes, the Knight and the Captain, fight with a shield or a guarded polearm, so their weapons additionally grant armor: their weapon is both their attack and part of their defense. Its shop-wide lines are Sharper Steel at level 4 (+1 power to every hero's tier 1+ weapon, per pip) and Keen Edge at level 5 (+2% crit chance to every hero's tier 1+ weapon, per pip).

The armor shop sells the armor slot — the chest piece, whether that is plate, a hauberk, leathers, or robes. Armor raises the defensive stats: the armor rating that subtracts flat damage, and at higher tiers the guard chance that blocks an attack outright. Its shop-wide lines are Thicker Plate at level 4 (+1 armor to every hero's tier 1+ armor, per pip) and Reinforced Straps at level 5 (+2% guard, per pip).

The jewelry shop sells the jewelry slot — a ring, an amulet, or a belt, depending on the class. Jewelry is the class-dependent slot: rather than granting the same stat to everyone, it amplifies whatever that class already wants to be doing. A Knight's belt makes him harder to hit through, a Berserker's ring makes his hits land harder, a Rogue's ring makes them land more often as crits, a Mage's amulet feeds its AOE, a Captain's amulet strengthens the Rallying Horn, and a Cleric's amulet makes its heal restore morale as well as health. The exact per-class effects are tabulated in LordofHirelings_BalanceNumbers.md. Its shop-wide lines are Warding Charms at level 4 (+2 max morale, per pip) and Soothing Stones at level 5 (+1 morale regen, per pip).

These six shop-wide lines are deliberately the strongest and most expensive thing in the game, which is why each sits at the end of its own shop's ladder rather than being available on day three. They are the payoff for investing in the town rather than in individuals.

The training shop is flavored as the training grounds. Here you can upgrade the training shop itself, improve hero experience gain rate, improve hero base offensive stats from level 0-5, improve hero base defensive stats from level 0-5 give heroes improved experience gain for 1-3 dungeons after they get hired, give heroes base xp when they get hired, and increase the amount of gold recovered from the dungeon. Note that this last upgrade increases the gold your adventurers pull out of the dungeon, since the player never receives dungeon gold directly — but because the tax-copy is a straight 10% of what they earn, it raises the player's income by the same 10% it raises theirs, and raises it again later as they spend more.

Name Generation
Every adventurer gets a randomly generated name and epithet, rolled independently and uniformly from two lists (see LordofHirelings_BalanceNumbers.md for the lists themselves). A generated pair must be unique among every adventurer the player can currently see referenced anywhere — living hires, hireable recruits at the inn, and the names on the graveyard's headstones — so if a roll collides with any of those, reroll until it does not. The lists are sized to make collisions rare rather than to make them impossible.

Item Naming
Equipment is named per class, per slot, and per tier, so a Knight's tier 3 weapon and a Mage's tier 3 weapon read completely differently in the inventory list even where they are mechanically similar. Tier 0 gear grants nothing mechanically but still has a name, because the inventory list always shows all three rows. The full 6-class × 6-tier × 3-slot naming table lives in LordofHirelings_BalanceNumbers.md.

Settings
Pressing Esc opens a pause menu with a settings screen. Opening it pauses everything, including mid-dive combat. The settings are the commonly expected ones — no visualization needed:
- Display: window mode (fullscreen, borderless, windowed), resolution, vsync.
- Audio: master, music, and sound effects volume sliders; a mute-when-window-unfocused toggle.
- Controls: rebindable keyboard keys for movement and interact (mouse buttons are fixed).
- Gameplay/accessibility: screen shake toggle, floating combat text toggle.
- A reset-to-defaults button. Settings are stored per install, separately from the save file, and apply immediately.

Title screen and campaign save
The title screen always shows New Game. It shows Continue only when the one campaign autosave exists. New Game starts a fresh campaign; when a campaign save exists, it first requires confirmation because it overwrites that save. Manage Save shows the current day, dungeon level, and most recent autosave point, and offers Delete Save behind its own confirmation. There are no save slots: the game intentionally has one replaceable campaign autosave.

Saving
The game automatically saves while the player is playing. There are exactly two autosave moments: right as the adventurers enter the dungeon, before any combat has happened at all, and again at the end of the expedition immediately before the expedition summary panel is shown. Launching the game resumes from the most recent autosave. Loading the second of those saves re-shows the expedition summary from the top, replaying its animations, so the player never loses the readout of a run they finished. Nothing is saved mid-combat: an adventurer dying does not trigger a save, and if the player quits mid-dive they resume from the start of that dive and the expedition is re-rolled. This is a deliberate simplification — it means combat state (initiative order, current HP and morale, fog position, spawned enemies, rolled gold traits, boss ability flags) never has to be serialized, and the save file only ever holds town state plus a "which dive are we about to run" marker. The same deliberate simplicity applies to town play: there is no autosave between the crow and the dungeon-entry save, so a player who hires and upgrades and then quits before entering the dungeon rolls back to the previous autosave. Saves are purposely coarse; do not add granularity.
